Home Inspections Just Don't Benefit Buyers
 

When we speak about real estate professionals we most often add the word “agent” without thinking fully about the legal implications of agency.

Webster’s Dictionary defines agent as “someone who represents a person in business; an intermediary.” When you decide it’s time to buy a home, you need to know that just because someone shows you a house, it doesn’t make him or her “your” agent.

Let’s look at “Agency” and its types.

  • Seller’s agent
    The seller creates an agency with the real estate person by signing a contract listing their house defining the price, terms desired and the length of the listing. The seller’s agent’s first obligation is to the seller and finding a buyer for them that meets the terms of the contract.
  • Buyer’s agent
    If you walk into a real estate office and tell the person at the desk you wish to purchase a home that action in itself doesn’t necessarily create a buyer’s agency. However, there are forms that you can sign that do create an agreement with the agent to find you a home that meets your needs. Retaining a buyer’s agent may give you an edge to finding the right home for you and getting it for a good price.
  • Dual agency
    This occurs when an agent represents both the seller and the buyer. The agent who listed the property will submit your offer to the seller. This is not legally or ethically wrong as long as it is fully disclosed and the agent walks the narrow line.

Many buyers may decide to use a “dual” agent in order to get a savings in commission. Since that agent doesn’t have to split the commission with another agent, they may be willing to get you a reduced price.

You do need to remember that whatever you might say to the listing agent most likely will end up in the seller’s ear.

Your agent - This gives only the basics about agency. Be sure to ask questions if you are in doubt about whom the agent is really representing.
